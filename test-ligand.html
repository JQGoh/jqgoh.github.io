Ligands on a Cluster

Attaching Ligands to a Cluster Effectively

Revised: April 5, 2017

Cluster science community has actively studied thiolates-protected gold clusters because of their interesting properties and potential applications. This type of cluster comprises of a metallic core with ligands covering it. Ligands, molecules attached to a cluster core, are important to decide the cluster properties. If we refer to Au25(SCH2CH2Ph)18- cluster, it has a metallic core of gold (Au13) icosahedron as shown below.


Visualizations of 3D molecules of this article are based on JSmol.
You could rotate the molecule, zoom-in or right-click to play with the other functionalities like measuring the distance between the atoms.

Together with six other ligands (each has a composiition of RS-Au-SR-Au-SR, with R=CH2CH2Ph), the Au13 icosahedron (highlighted in lightgray) with the ligands attached give us the complete structure of Au25(SCH2CH2Ph)18- cluster below.

The ligand is attached to the gold icosahedral core surface by connecting their two sulfur atoms (yellow spheres) with two surface gold atoms. Attaching ligands to a cluster core manually by using a molecular visualizer (such as Jmol) is a repetitive task. In some cases, we are interested to study problem such as the catalytic behaviour of oxygen molecule at the surface of cluster. We may need to arrange the oxygen molecules such that they are properly aligned closer to the cluster surface. What if we need to attach tens of oxygen molecules to the cluster surface? This can be a boring/demanding tasks for users trying to position the ligands by moving/clicking with their computer pointers.

Using the  Atomic Simulation Environment package, I designed a programme that can automate the process of attaching ligands to the surface of a cluster. The automation process assumes that a ligand is attached to only two surface atoms, marked by the sites (green dots) as shown below. If a ligand has occupied the sites marked by green dots, another ligand will not be able to occupy the sites marked by red dots as it will overlap with the existing ligand.

The designed programme can

  1. Determine the surface atoms of a cluster core.
  2. Thereafter, automatically attach a given ligand molecule (with pre-defined orientation) to the surface of the cluster.

Here is the result of attaching ligands to an icosahedral gold cluster using the programme. This helps to save my effort in studying the effect of ligands orientation on cluster cores of different sizes and shapes, whenever I try to study a new system with ligands of different orientations. The example code of this post and the main programme are available on my GitHub page.
